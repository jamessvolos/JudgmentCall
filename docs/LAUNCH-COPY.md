13 preregistered calls all read "JURY'S OUT" until a contrast clears n≥30, so
every format below points at the ~90-second voting loop, not the feature list.

Live: **https://judgment-call.vercel.app**
Voice note: this is a product about not overclaiming — the copy stays honest.
No "revolutionary," no fake urgency. Let the premise do the work.

## 4 · Newsletter / community drop (longer)

> **Can you tell when a data story goes one step too far?**
>
> Judgment Call is a live experiment about the exact moment a finding's *writing*
> outruns its *data* — every number accurate, the sentence still overclaiming.
>
> How it works: you see one finding told two ways and pick the better telling.
> Ten head-to-heads, ~90 seconds, completely blind. At the end you get a read on
> your own taste — numbers-first or implication-first, terse or thorough,
> precise or rounded — and, more interestingly, **where you and the crowd
> disagree.**
>
> The twist: the house desk has preregistered 13 opinionated calls about what
> makes a telling land, dated and frozen before the votes arrived. The crowd
> grades them live and can overrule the desk in public — and because the calls
> were locked in advance, roughly one verdict in thirteen should eventually be
> wrong by chance, which the desk says out loud before it happens.
>
> There's also a Training Room if you want to sharpen the reflex: spot the
> overreach, audit a claim ledger line by line, sit a checkpoint. But you don't
> need any of that to help — just cast ten calls.
>
> **→ https://judgment-call.vercel.app**

## 6 · The "why should I care" paragraph (for a reply / about page)

> Most advice about data storytelling is vibes. This is an attempt to measure it:
> thousands of blind pairwise calls, tagged by what actually differed between the
> two tellings — lead structure, length, precision, where the caveat sits — so
> the preferences fall out of the votes instead of someone's opinion. The desk
> stakes out positions in advance so it can be caught being wrong. That's the
> whole point.

## Distribution notes (for you, not for posting)

- **Best-fit audiences:** data analysts/scientists, analytics-adjacent PMs and
  marketers, journalism/dataviz communities, stats and DS courses. The premise
  lands hardest with people who *write* findings, not just read them.
- **The share cards already work:** each desk call has its own page
  (`/calls/1`…`/calls/13`) with a per-call OG image, and personal results have a
  `/p/[slug]` permalink with a poster — so any link you drop unfurls with a real
  card, no extra work.
- **What "success" looks like:** the goal is n≥30 on the busiest contrasts, at
  which point the first desk verdicts flip from "JURY'S OUT" to CONCURS/OVERRULES
  and /results becomes genuinely alive. Concentrated bursts (one newsletter, one
  active Slack) beat a slow trickle for crossing that threshold.
- **A hook that reliably converts:** lead with the *disagreement* promise
  ("where your taste is contrarian"), not the study framing — people click to
  find out about themselves, then stay for the desk.
